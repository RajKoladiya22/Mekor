Material

Overview of polymer materials, their properties and advantages vs. metal alternatives.

Overview

Plastics used for piping are primarily thermoplastics. They are made from long-chain polymer molecules and are categorized by molecular structure and performance into semicrystalline and amorphous polymers. Material choice affects welding, chemical resistance, thermal behaviour and lifecycle emissions.

Common material families

  • Polyethylene (PE) — flexible, good impact resistance; used in mains and drainage.
  • Polypropylene (PP / PPR) — higher temperature resistance; common for plumbing and hot-water.
  • Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC / PVC-U / PVC-C) — rigid pipe solutions, good chemical resistance.
  • Polyphenylene (PPH) — for aggressive chemical lines and process piping.
  • Fluoropolymers — specialised, high chemical and temperature resistance.

Material selection rules

  1. Match polymer chemistry to fluid and temperature.
  2. Choose semicrystalline polymers for hot welding and pressure stability.
  3. Choose amorphous polymers for solvent cementing and tight dimensional tolerance.
  4. Consider lifecycle carbon footprint and recyclability for sustainable projects.

Advantages vs metal

  • Lower density and easier handling
  • Excellent corrosion and chemical resistance
  • Lower thermal conductivity reducing heat loss
  • Simpler, faster jointing methods and reduced installation cost

Quick comparison — Metal vs Plastic

Property Metal Plastic
Density / Weight High — heavy handling, cranes often needed Low — hand-portable for many sizes
Thermal conductivity High — needs insulation to limit losses Low — inherent insulation, less heat loss
Corrosion Subject to galvanic and chemical corrosion High corrosion resistance — no galvanic issues
Chemical resistance Varies; surface treatments required Often excellent when correct polymer selected
Installation Heavy, requires anchoring and welding/fittings Faster jointing, smaller fixings, lower labour cost
